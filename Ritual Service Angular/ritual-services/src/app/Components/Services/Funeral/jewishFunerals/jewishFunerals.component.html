Jewish Funeral

The idea of ​​the Jews about death

In Judaism there is no familiar concept of "hell" or "heaven". The Torah and the Talmud (sacred Jewish books) point out that there is no sin for which the soul must suffer forever, the afterlife of the soul who committed the crime is rather a process of cleansing from sins, but not punishment. In Judaism there is also the concept of resurrection from the dead, it must take place after the coming of the Messiah (messiah). In this, the dogmas of Judaism are fundamentally different from Orthodoxy, where the sinful soul, going to hell, is tormented for his sins.

Preparation for death

Before death, the dying person must recite the Vedui (list all his sins and repent of them). This custom brings Judaism closer to Orthodoxy, which requires the last visit of the dying Orthodox priest and repentance of sins.

The moment of death

Based on the dogmas of Judaism, the deceased should be buried almost on the day of death, in extreme cases within two days. Relatives must do everything so that the deceased is not dissected, his body must remain intact (this is done in light of the resurrection from the dead with the coming of the messiah). On the day of death, relatives do not eat meat, do not drink wine, do not work, do not read daily prayers. Funerals are not held on Shabbat (Saturday). The Jewish tradition also provides for mourning, burial of the deceased, accompanying him on his last journey.

Washing, dressing and placing in the coffin of the body of the deceased

Jews are buried in the shroud, not in the coffin, but if the coffin is used, it is always closed. Any coffin is used. The body is washed and dressed in white clothes. In the Jewish tradition, special prayers are also recited and the deceased is buried.

Burial

Funeral participants and close relatives tear their clothes as a sign of great sorrow (Kria rite). The coffin is carried out by special religious ministers of Hevra Kaddish, relatives go out with them until they all pass, it is forbidden to enter the mourning hall. At the cemetery, the son of the deceased reads a prayer intended for this occasion - Kaddish, if there is no son, then Kaddish is recited by a respected member of the community. Everyone throws a handful of earth into the grave, this tradition unites Judaism with Orthodox funerals.

Funeral

Funerals for Jews are held in the same way as for Orthodox. Relatives who bring food with them gather (round rolls and eggs are obligatory).
